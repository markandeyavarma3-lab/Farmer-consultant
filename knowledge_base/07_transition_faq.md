# Transitioning to Natural Farming — Common Questions

> Plain-language answers to the questions farmers most often ask when starting out. Keep
> answers honest: the transition takes time and the first year or two can be hard.

## "Will my yield drop if I stop using chemicals?"

Possibly, in the first 1-2 years, while the soil's natural biology rebuilds after years of
chemical use. This is normal and temporary. The honest picture: your **costs fall immediately**
(no more buying fertiliser and pesticide), and over a few years soil health and yields
typically recover, while your *net income* often improves because you are spending so much
less. This is why APCNF advises starting on a **small trial plot**, not your whole field.

## "How do I start without risking everything?"

Start on a small portion of your land — even a quarter or half acre. Learn the inputs
(Jeevamrutham, Beejamrutham), watch how the soil and crop respond over one full season, then
expand. Keep your main field as it is until you are confident. APCNF's whole model is a
gradual 3-5 year transition with hand-holding from a Community Resource Person.

## "I don't have a desi (indigenous) cow. Can I still do natural farming?"

Desi cow dung and urine are preferred because their microbial population is richer, but you
can begin with whatever local cattle dung/urine you can access, or share with a neighbour who
has cattle. One cow's dung/urine can support inputs for roughly 30 acres, so a few households
can share one animal.

## "Is natural farming the same as organic farming?"

No. Organic farming often still buys external inputs (bought organic manures, bio-pesticides,
permitted sprays) and can be input-intensive and costly. Natural farming aims for **near-zero
external purchase** — almost all inputs are made on the farm from cow dung, urine, and local
plants. That is why it is called "zero budget" in Palekar's framing.

## "How much money will I save?"

The biggest, most reliable saving is on **purchased inputs** — chemical fertiliser and
pesticide, which can be 50-70% of a conventional farmer's cultivation cost, drop to almost
nothing. Water and electricity use also fall. Do not expect instant higher yields; expect
much lower spending, which is what improves income.

## "Who can help me near my village?"

The best help is a local **APCNF / RySS Community Resource Person (CRP)** — an experienced
natural farmer trained to guide you. You can also call the **Kisan Call Centre at
1800-180-1551** (6 AM-10 PM, Telugu and Hindi available) or visit your district's **Krishi
Vigyan Kendra (KVK)**. This voice assistant can explain methods and recipes, but it cannot
visit your field — for anything needing a field visit or local judgement, ask a CRP.
