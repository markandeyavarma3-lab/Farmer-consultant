# Multi-Layer / Five-Layer Cropping Model (Panchasthariya)

> This is the domain meaning of "multilevel" in natural farming: growing crops of different
> heights together so sunlight, space, and soil are used at every level — like a natural forest.
> Sources: Subhash Palekar's Five-Layer (Panchasthariya) model and APCNF cropping models.

## Why grow in layers?

A natural forest has no bare ground and no chemical inputs, yet it is lush and productive,
because plants of many heights share the same space — tall trees, medium trees, shrubs,
ground crops, and climbers all capture sunlight at different levels and root at different
depths. Multi-layer farming copies this. Benefits:

- **Maximum use of sunlight** — every ray hits some leaf.
- **Continuous income** — short crops give quick returns while long crops mature.
- **Risk spreading** — if one crop fails (pest, price, weather), others still yield.
- **Soil protection** — full ground cover prevents erosion and moisture loss (Whapasa).
- **Natural pest control** — diversity confuses pests and shelters their predators.

## The Five Layers (Palekar model)

A commonly taught arrangement, e.g. on a 36 ft x 36 ft unit, layering by height:

1. **Layer 1 — Tall canopy (longest term):** coconut, areca nut, mango, tall timber/fruit
   trees. These take years to mature but give the highest long-term value.
2. **Layer 2 — Medium trees:** sapota (chikoo), guava, lemon, custard apple, drumstick.
3. **Layer 3 — Shrubs / bushes:** curry leaf, coffee, tea, pigeon pea (red gram), papaya.
4. **Layer 4 — Ground / herb layer (shortest term, quickest cash):** vegetables, leafy
   greens, turmeric, ginger, pulses, groundnut — harvested in weeks to months.
5. **Layer 5 — Climbers and underground:** gourds/beans climbing the trees above, plus
   tuber crops (yam, sweet potato, colocasia) using the soil below.

The short-duration ground crops (Layer 4) pay the bills in the first months and years while
the trees (Layers 1-2) grow into their high-value maturity.

## APCNF cropping models (Andhra Pradesh)

APCNF promotes several named, ready-to-use diverse models:

- **365 Days Green Cover (365 DGC):** the umbrella goal — never leave soil bare; always have
  living crops, enabled by Pre-Monsoon Dry Sowing.
- **"A Grade" model:** 5 main crops grown together with 20-25 additional biodiversity crops
  (border crops, trap crops, flowering crops for pollinators).
- **"ATM" model (Any Time Money):** a dense ~0.20 acre (20 cents) plot with 15-18 crops of
  staggered maturity so the family has *something to harvest and sell almost any week* — built
  for landless and marginal farmers. The name captures the idea: the plot works like an ATM.
- **Five-layer horticulture model:** a 100 sq m base unit with trenches, layering horticulture
  crops as above.

## How the assistant should advise on this

- Start by asking the farmer's land size, water availability, and how soon they need income.
- A marginal farmer needing quick cash -> suggest the **ATM model** or a strong Layer-4 mix.
- A farmer with some land and patience for trees -> introduce the **five-layer model** gradually.
- Never tell a farmer to convert their whole field at once. APCNF advises starting on a small
  trial plot, learning, then expanding over 3-5 years.
